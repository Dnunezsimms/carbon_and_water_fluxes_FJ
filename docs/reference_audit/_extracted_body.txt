Carbon and water fluxes reveal that one of the world’s most arid shrublands functions as a net carbon sink

Abstract
Climate change is reshaping carbon and water fluxes across ecosystems worldwide and yet widespread, systems such as arid shrublands remain understudied. We quantified carbon and water fluxes using continuous eddy covariance measurements during three years in one of the world’s most arid shrublands in northern Chile and estimated these fluxes spatially using remote-sensing data. The ecosystem exhibited marked ecohydrological variability driven by episodic rainfall pulses and acted as a persistent net carbon sink, with annual net ecosystem exchange (NEE) ranging from −187 to −305 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹, while evapotranspiration ranged from 182 to 257 mm yr⁻¹ and was always higher than precipitation (66-184 mm yr-1), suggesting possible access to deeper water sources, including deep soil moisture and potential groundwater influence. A Support Vector Regression (SVR) model integrating Sentinel-2 vegetation indices and terrain-adjusted radiation predicted NEE with an R² of 0.56 and estimated mean landscape values of −188 ± 31 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹ (−6.9 ± 1.1 Mg CO₂ ha⁻¹ yr⁻¹). Carbon balance was primarily controlled by radiation and modulated by soil water content and vapor pressure deficit. Predictive models showed a good level of agreement with tower observations, supporting their representativeness of the shrubland at the landscape scale. These findings indicate that arid shrublands can act as carbon sinks across multiple growing seasons and are regulated mainly by hydroclimatic pulses, highlighting the ecological importance of arid shrublands and the need for their conservation in South America and elsewhere.

Introduction
Climate change represents one of the most critical global environmental challenges, primarily driven by anthropogenic greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, such as carbon dioxide (CO2), resulting from fossil fuel combustion, deforestation and land-use changes (IPCC, 2023). GHG accumulation in the atmosphere increases global temperatures and alters essential carbon and water fluxes, affecting terrestrial ecosystems functioning, biodiversity, and the ecosystem services that support agriculture, water availability, and resilience to extreme climatic events (Sintayehu, 2018). Arid ecosystems, covering approximately 40% of the Earth’s surface, play a crucial role in climate regulation despite their limited carbon storage capacity due to water scarcity, making them a research and conservation priority (Bastin et al., 2017).
In arid ecosystems, CO2 exchange between the atmosphere and land strongly depends on water availability in the root zone, which regulates photosynthesis and respiration (Dralle et al., 2020). Episodic rainfall pulses activate key biophysical processes and strongly modulate ecosystem carbon fluxes in dryland systems (Jia et al., 2014). In parallel, the interaction between vegetation structure and geomorphology controls water redistribution across the landscape, with implications for long-term ecosystem functioning (Sabathier et al., 2021). Extreme climatic events such as heatwaves can substantially alter evapotranspiration and plant physiological processes, with cascading effects on ecosystem carbon exchange (Piao et al., 2019). In contrast, intense short-duration rainfall pulses rapidly stimulate soil microbial activity, producing strong but transient responses in carbon cycling and ecosystem functioning (Collins et al., 2014). Identifying critical thresholds of environmental variables, particularly soil moisture and temperature, is therefore essential to evaluate ecosystem resilience (Fu et al., 2022).
Despite the central role of water and carbon fluxes in arid ecosystems, our understanding of soil– vegetation–atmosphere interactions remains limited in many regions (Perez-Quezada et al., 2023a). These environments are strongly shaped by the episodic nature of rainfall and soil moisture pulses, which regulate plant activity and carbon exchange but remain challenging to capture with conventional approaches (Reynolds et al., 2004; Huxman et al., 2004). Sparse and heterogeneous vegetation cover, together with pronounced soil variability, further complicates the quantification and modeling of these ecohydrological processes (Whitford and Duval, 2019; Rodríguez-Iturbe and Porporato, 2005). Moreover, persistent uncertainty in soil–climate feedbacks and the role of water availability in driving carbon balance highlights the need for integrative approaches to assess ecosystem functioning under climate change scenarios (Seneviratne et al., 2010; Biederman et al., 2017; Humphrey et al., 2021).

To characterize productivity and water availability in arid ecosystems, remote-sensing indices such as the Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI), Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), and Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) are widely employed (Gao, 1996; da Silva et al., 2017; de la Maza et al., 2008). These remote-sensing indicators are often complemented with direct measurements from eddy covariance towers, allowing for a more comprehensive assessment of carbon and water dynamics in dryland ecosystems (Biederman et al., 2017). Building on this integration, machine learning approaches, including Random Forest and Support Vector Regression, have been increasingly applied to predict net ecosystem exchange (NEE) in semi-arid landscapes, demonstrating their ability to capture complex and nonlinear ecological processes (Zhou et al., 2019).
Specifically, our objectives were (1) to quantify annual carbon and water fluxes using eddy covariance measurements, (2) to identify the key climatic drivers and thresholds controlling these fluxes, and (3) to develop and validate a remote sensing–based predictive model to upscale tower-derived net ecosystem exchange (NEE) and quantify carbon balance at the landscape scale in a coastal arid thorn scrub ecosystem.
Materials and Methods
Study area and site descriptions
The study was conducted in the Long-Term Socio-Ecological Research (LTSER) site in Bosque Fray Jorge National Park (PNBFJ) and its surrounding areas, located in the Coquimbo Region of northern Chile. The park covers 9,959 ha and represents a unique ecological refuge where plant formations persist under microclimatic conditions influenced by coastal fog, locally called camanchaca (CONAF, 2014; Meserve et al., 2016). In this sector, an eddy covariance (EC) flux tower was installed in July 2022 to monitor carbon and water fluxes in a shrubland with succulents land-cover unit, as defined by the official vegetation maps of the Chilean National Forestry Corporation (Corporacion Nacional Forestal, CONAF) (Fig. 1) (coordinates 30◦39′29.54′′ S, 71◦39′50.73′′ W). The site is registered in AmeriFlux as CL-FJS (Fray Jorge Shrubland).

Throughout this study, a distinction is made between the ecological characterization of the ecosystem under study and the cartographic land cover classes used for spatial analyses. Although the site is ecologically described as a thorn scrub based on vegetation structure and species composition, spatial filtering and modeling are based on CONAF's official land cover classification, using the category “Shrubland with Succulent” as the operational cartographic unit (Fig. 1).
Fray Jorge National Park exhibits a climate characterized by Mediterranean-type precipitation seasonality, shaped by strong coastal influences from the Pacific Anticyclone and the Humboldt Current, as well as complex local topography (Meserve et al., 2016). Long-term climatic records indicate a mean annual precipitation of 126.8 mm for the period 1913–2013 (Meteorological Directorate of Chile, 2013; Armas et al., 2016), which declined markedly to 81.6 mm during the 2013–2025 period (CEAZA, 2025), with most precipitation concentrated in brief rainfall events between May and September. In addition to rainfall, coastal fog (camanchaca) represents an important water input, reaching approximately 600–800 mm yr⁻¹ in exposed areas of the coastal range, particularly in fog-dependent forests, but decreasing markedly in leeward areas due to topographic effects (Kummerow, 1966; Cruzat-Gallardo, 2004). At the eddy covariance tower location, site-level extraction from the Global Aridity Index and Potential Evapotranspiration Database (v3; 1970–2000) yielded an Aridity Index of 0.084 (Zomer et al., 2022; see Figure S1 in the Supplementary Material), well below the 0.20 threshold defining arid environments (FAO, 2006). Despite this Mediterranean-type precipitation regime, water inputs remain low relative to atmospheric demand, resulting in arid conditions at the ecosystem scale.
Intra-annual and inter-annual variability in the system is characterized by high hydroclimatic variability and episodic precipitation pulses, resulting in pronounced fluctuations in water availability. While large-scale climate modes such as the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) can influence regional atmospheric circulation, precipitation in the study area does not exhibit a consistent or deterministic association with ENSO phases. Based on daily observations, mean monthly air temperature ranges from approximately 11 °C in July (coldest month) to 17.8 °C in February (warmest month), with an overall mean annual temperature of ~14 °C. Photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) and soil temperature (TS) peak during periods of maximum energy input. Annual precipitation shows strong interannual variability relative to the 40-year mean, with years of effective hydrological recharge (>300–400 mm) and extremely dry years (<50 mm) occurring under different large-scale climatic contexts (Fig. 2). The observation period, which spans July 2022 to June 2025 and overlaps calendar years 2022–2025, is highlighted in Fig. 2 to place the flux measurements within this long-term precipitation context. This pulse-driven hydroclimatic variability underpins the ecohydrological dynamics that regulate carbon and water fluxes in arid shrublands.

Soils at the study site were characterized using two representative profiles excavated to approximately 1.2 m depth at valley-bottom and mid-slope positions, developed over Pleistocene–Holocene colluvial–alluvial deposits. The valley-bottom soil was classified as a Typic Haplocambids (Aridisol, Cambids), with an ochric epipedon overlying a weakly developed cambic (Bw) horizon, coarse sandy to sandy loam textures, high gravel content, and imperfect to moderately well drainage; redoximorphic features in deeper horizons indicate episodic saturation and the influence of a shallow, temporally variable water table (Soil Survey Staff, 2022). The mid-slope soil corresponds to an Arenic Haplargids (Aridisol, Argids), characterized by an ochric epipedon and a well-developed argillic (Bt) horizon, sandy loam to sandy clay loam textures, moderate plasticity at depth, and moderately well-drained conditions (Soil Survey Staff, 2022). Across both profiles, bulk density ranged from 0.96 to 1.26 g cm⁻³, total porosity from 52 to 64%, and organic matter content from 0.83 to 4.67%, reflecting coarse textures, limited aggregation, and decreasing organic inputs with depth. Regional hydrogeological models indicate groundwater depths of approximately 2–10 m near valley bottoms and ephemeral channels. Soil water content (SWC) was monitored at 5 cm depth using Hydra Probe II sensors (three replicates per site), and Relative Extractable Water (REW) was calculated from laboratory-determined field capacity (−33 kPa) and permanent wilting point (−1500 kPa) following Granier et al. (2007); together with fog-derived moisture inputs and perennial vegetation, these edaphic and hydrological conditions exert a strong control on near-surface water availability and the observed ecosystem carbon and water flux responses ((see Table S1 and Figure S2 in the Supplementary Material). For more details about geomorphological structure of the study site see Figure S3.
Within the area relevant to the eddy covariance flux footprint, arid thorn scrub vegetation of 2-3 m in height is the predominant vegetation formation, covering 56.7% of Bosque Fray Jorge National Park (Fig. 1a). Characteristic thorn scrub species include the evergreen Porlieria chilensis and drought-deciduous shrubs such as Adesmia bedwellii and Proustia cuneifolia (Squeo et al., 2016; Gutierrez et al., 1993). This formation also hosts a large diversity of ephemeral and geophyte species (Fernandez-Murillo et al., 2023), whose development depends strongly on winter rainfall and on the occurrence of sufficiently wet years. In the fog-free coastal desert, ephemeral cover may fail to develop in very dry years with less than ~20 mm of annual rainfall (Armesto et al., 1993). Perennial shrubs act as nurse plants, creating microhabitats with higher soil moisture and organic matter, facilitating the establishment of herbaceous species during rainy periods, particularly in degraded coastal communities (Armesto et al., 1993). The flux footprint was filtered to exclude areas influenced by non-native vegetation and human infrastructure (Fig. 1b).
Meteorological and ancillary data
Climatic variables were continuously monitored at the flux tower and recorded every 30 minutes. Photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) was measured using a quantum sensor (model LI-190R, LI-COR Biosciences, Lincoln, NE, USA). Air temperature and relative humidity were recorded with a probe (model HMP155, Vaisala, Helsinki, Finland). Vapor pressure deficit (VPD) was derived from measured air temperature and relative humidity following the Magnus formulation (Sonntag, 1990). Precipitation was measured with a tipping-bucket rain gauge (model TR-525M, Texas Electronics, Dallas, TX, USA). Soil heat flux was determined using three heat flux plates (model HFP01-15; Hukseflux Thermal Sensors, Delft, The Netherlands), and four-component radiation was measured with a radiometer (CNR4; Kipp & Zonen, Delft, The Netherlands). Data acquisition and system control were managed using a SmartFlux module (SmartFlux 3, model 7900-610, LI-COR), a data acquisition unit (LI-7900-110, LI-COR), and a main control box (LI-7900-126, LI-COR).
To evaluate the ecohydrological functioning of the desert shrubland in Bosque Fray Jorge National Park, the study period was divided into three ecohydrological years spanning from July to June: Year 1 (July 2022–June 2023), Year 2 (July 2023–June 2024), and Year 3 (July 2024–June 2025). By contrast, to better represent water availability relevant to ecosystem functioning, we defined annual effective precipitation operationally as the accumulated rainfall from March of one year to March of the following year, rather than strictly within the July-to-June ecohydrological year. This aggregation window was selected because late autumn and early winter rainfall can contribute to soil water recharge and influence vegetation activity and ecosystem carbon and water fluxes during the subsequent growing season. We therefore use the term effective precipitation in an operational ecohydrological sense, rather than as a strict hydrological estimate of plant-available water after runoff, evaporation, or drainage losses. Gaps in meteorological variables required for flux post-processing were filled using linear regression approaches with ERA5 reanalysis data as predictors (Hersbach et al., 2020). Daily, annual, and multi-year summaries of meteorological variables were subsequently derived and analyzed using Python 3.13 (Python Software Foundation, 2023).
Carbon and water fluxes
Eddy covariance (EC) measurements were conducted continuously from July 2022 to June 2025 using a 7 m tower equipped with a three-dimensional sonic anemometer (WindMaster Pro, Gill Instruments Ltd., Lymington, UK) and an open-path infrared gas analyzer (LI-7542; LI-COR Biosciences, Lincoln, NE, USA). Raw half-hourly fluxes were processed using EddyPro software (October 2025 release; LI-COR Biosciences), including coordinate rotation, spectral corrections, WPL correction, and time-lag compensation, following standard procedures (Fratini and Mauder, 2014).
A multi-step post-processing workflow was applied. First, quality screening was performed based on physically plausible ranges, instrument diagnostic flags, and inter-variable dependencies following Mauder and Foken (2004). Additionally, CO₂ fluxes were filtered to retain values within a range of −30 to 30 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹, and only measurements with infrared gas analyzer (IRGA) signal strength greater than 70% were retained. Fluxes were then filtered to exclude measurements originating outside the target ecosystem using a footprint filtering approach based on fetch distance (FETCH₉₀) and wind direction, removing approximately 48.6% of the data. Low-turbulence conditions were further filtered using a friction velocity (u*) threshold estimated with a bootstrap approach (Papale et al., 2006), resulting in an additional removal of approximately 15.6% of the data. In total, about 66% of the original dataset was excluded after all filtering steps.
Gap-filling of NEE, sensible heat (H), and latent heat flux (LE) was performed using a machine learning approach based on bagging regression (BaggingRegressor). The model used incoming shortwave radiation (SWin), maximum wind speed (MWS), outgoing longwave radiation (LWout), soil temperature (TS), net radiation (NETRAD), air temperature (Ta), and sensible heat flux (H) as predictors, achieving an R² of 0.7. For comparison and consistency with established methodologies, marginal distribution sampling (MDS) was also considered following FLUXNET and ICOS Recommendations (Reichstein et al., 2005; Papale et al., 2006; Siegel et al., 2021).
Energy balance closure was assessed using the energy balance ratio (EBR), calculated from daily sums of turbulent fluxes and available energy using non-gap-filled data under daytime conditions, yielding a value of 0.78, within the expected range for eddy covariance measurements.
Gross primary productivity (GPP) and ecosystem respiration (Reco) were derived from gap-filled NEE using both the nighttime-based approach of Reichstein et al. (2005) and the light-response curve method of Lasslop et al. (2010). Evapotranspiration (ET) was estimated from latent heat flux (LE) at 30-minute intervals (Stull, 1988) and partitioned into transpiration (T) and evaporation (E) using the Transpiration Estimation Algorithm (TEA; Nelson et al., 2020).

Drivers of carbon and water fluxes
Environmental drivers (soil water content, SWC; relative extractable water, REW; air temperature, TA; soil temperature, TS; incoming shortwave radiation, PAR; incoming longwave radiation, LW_IN; precipitation, P_RAIN; and vapor pressure deficit, VPD) were explored using pairwise Pearson correlations with the target fluxes (NEE, GPP, Reco, ET, T, and E). To identify key drivers and abrupt thresholds in daily ecosystem functioning, we applied Classification and Regression Trees (CART), a non-parametric method that partitions responses into hierarchical combinations of predictors and captures nonlinear responses and threshold behavior in ecohydrological systems (Breiman et al., 1984; De’ath and Fabricius, 2000). CART has been successfully applied to identify controls on carbon and water fluxes across contrasting climatic and edaphic contexts (Li et al., 2020; Perez-Quezada et al., 2023b).
CART models were developed for net ecosystem exchange (NEE), evapotranspiration (ET), transpiration (T), and evaporation (E), using the selected environmental drivers as predictors. The daily CART analyses were based on processed Level 3 products using quality-controlled and gap-filled flux and meteorological series, including NEE_F2, ET_F, and filled VPD, and SWC variables. This approach improved temporal continuity for threshold analysis, but may also partially smooth relationships among predictors and response variables. Tree splits were based on information gain, and model complexity was constrained (cp = 0.025, minsplit = 100, maxdepth = 2) to reduce overfitting. Models were implemented in RStudio using rpart (RStudio Team, 2022; Therneau and Atkinson, 2019) and in SPYDER using scikit-learn (Raybaut and Team, 2023; Python Software Foundation, 2023; Pedregosa et al., 2011) and evaluated through 10-fold cross-validation.
Identified split thresholds and node statistics were examined alongside annual aggregates and time series to interpret patterns of coupling and decoupling between carbon and water fluxes, with particular emphasis on variations in water-use efficiency under contrasting edaphoclimatic conditions. Graphical outputs included pruned decision trees, boxplots, and comparative time series.
Remote sensing and spatial estimation of NEE
The satellite predictors used to estimate net ecosystem exchange (NEE) consisted of Sentinel-2 vegetation and moisture indices (NDVI, EVI, NDWI), Landsat 8/9 land surface temperature (LST), and Sentinel-1 radar–derived soil moisture proxies (SWC_norm and Pol_moisture). In addition, terrain-adjusted radiation (RAD_topo) was derived from a digital elevation model to account for topographic effects on incoming radiation. All datasets were harmonized to a spatial resolution of 30 m and temporally matched to daily eddy covariance (EC) flux observations (Table 1). Scenes with more than 20% cloud cover were excluded. Predictor redundancy was minimized by filtering predictor combinations to exclude highly correlated variables (|r| > 0.1).
Predictor time series were processed at the pixel level, preserving the original temporal sampling of each sensor. Model predictions of NEE were generated for all available observation dates per pixel. Spatially, pixel-level annual NEE metrics (accumulated, mean, and standard deviation of daily NEE) were mapped directly onto geographic coordinates without spatial interpolation or smoothing, preserving the native spatial structure of the predictions.
Machine learning models, including Random Forest, Support Vector Regression (SVR), and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), were evaluated due to their ability to capture nonlinear relationships and interactions among predictors (Breiman, 2001; Friedman, 2001; Tibshirani, 1996). Linear regression was included as a baseline model. Model performance was assessed using leave-one-season-out (LOSO) cross-validation to ensure temporal generalization (Roberts et al., 2017), using , RMSE, and MAE as evaluation metrics.
Based on model performance and ecological interpretability, two model configurations were retained for spatial prediction: (i) a statistically optimal model based on NDVI and RAD_topo, and (ii) an ecohydrologically constrained model including NDVI, RAD_topo, and Pol_moisture to explicitly account for moisture controls on ecosystem carbon fluxes. Predictions were applied only to pixels classified as succulent shrubland in the CONAF map, under the working assumption that this land-cover class is sufficiently homogeneous at the model scale to support within-class extrapolation from the eddy covariance tower.

Results
Carbon and water flux responses to ecohydrological variability
Results revealed pronounced interannual variability in ecosystem water fluxes associated with differences in effective precipitation, soil water availability, and evapotranspiration (Fig. 3). Year 1 received 90.1 mm of effective precipitation (see Table S3) and was characterized by two marked winter rainfall pulses (>20 mm day⁻¹) in July. Soil water content (SWC) showed short-lived increases, with an annual median of 0.033 m³ m⁻³ and SWC >0.15 m³ m⁻³ occurring during 2.5% of the ecohydrological year. Evapotranspiration (ET) remained low during winter and early spring (<1.2 mm day⁻¹).

Figure 3. Daily dynamics of vapor pressure deficit, evapotranspiration, soil water content, and precipitation for ecohydrological years 1–3.
Year 2 had the lowest effective precipitation (65.9 mm) and the lowest median SWC (0.026 m³ m⁻³). Despite isolated high-intensity rainfall events, SWC >0.15 m³ m⁻³ occurred during 8.2% of the year, while ET remained moderate to low (1.0–1.4 mm day⁻¹). Year 3 received the highest effective precipitation (183.7 mm) and exhibited the highest median SWC (0.044 m³ m⁻³) and integrated soil water availability. Annual ET totaled 257 mm, and the fraction of days with ET >1.2 mm day⁻¹ doubled relative to Years 1 and 2. Transpiration accounted for 55.6%, 50.7%, and 62.1% of total ET in Years 1–3, respectively (Fig. 4).
Median vapor pressure deficit was similar across years, and no days exceeded 1.5 kPa.

Marked interannual differences in effective precipitation, soil water availability, and carbon fluxes were observed across the three ecohydrological years (Fig. 5; Table S3). Year 1 (2022–2023) was characterized by relatively low total precipitation (90.2 mm) and moderate rainfall events (maximum daily precipitation = 25.2 mm). Despite low average relative extractable water (REW = 0.13), gross primary productivity (GPP, plotted as positive by convention) reached a mean of 2.33 g C m⁻² d⁻¹ and a maximum of 6.02 g C m⁻² d⁻¹, accumulating to 840 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹. Ecosystem respiration (RECO) averaged 1.57 g C m⁻² d⁻¹ and accumulated to 569 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹, resulting in a net annual carbon uptake of −279 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹.
Year 2 (2023–2024) was characterized by relatively low accumulated precipitation (66 mm), but comparatively higher mean soil water availability (REW = 0.25), indicating the occurrence of short-lived moisture pulses. Under these conditions, mean and maximum GPP (2.14 and 4.94 g C m⁻² d⁻¹, respectively) and annual GPP (765 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹) were lower than in Year 1. RECO remained relatively stable (578 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹), while net carbon uptake was reduced (NEE = −187 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹). This pattern suggests that the temporal distribution and persistence of soil moisture pulses played a key role in regulating ecosystem productivity. In contrast, Year 3 (2024–2025) showed higher effective precipitation (184 mm) and included larger recharge events (maximum daily precipitation = 65.7 mm), resulting in moderate soil water availability (REW = 0.17). These conditions supported enhanced photosynthetic activity, with GPP reaching a mean of 2.56 g C m⁻² d⁻¹ and a maximum of 5.91 g C m⁻² d⁻¹, and the highest annual accumulation (915 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹). Although RECO also increased (610 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹), the ecosystem functioned as a stronger carbon sink (NEE = −305 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹).

Across years, photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) showed relatively low interannual variability compared to hydrological drivers, indicating that differences in carbon exchange were primarily associated with precipitation patterns and soil water dynamics rather than changes in incoming radiation.

Functional drivers and thresholds of carbon and water fluxes
Results showed that net ecosystem CO₂ exchange (NEE) was positively associated with soil water content (SWC) and relative extractable water (REW), and negatively related to photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), while temperature effects were weak (Fig. 7). Gross primary productivity (GPP) was primarily driven by PAR, with weak negative relationships with soil moisture variables.
Ecosystem respiration (Reco) exhibited strong negative correlations with temperature and PAR, and positive associations with SWC and REW. These patterns suggest that apparent relationships with energy-related variables may reflect indirect coupling with soil moisture conditions, highlighting water availability as the dominant control over respiration dynamics in this arid system.
Water fluxes displayed differentiated responses, while evaporation (E) was strongly linked to SWC and REW, evapotranspiration (ET) showed moderate associations with these variables, and transpiration (T) exhibited generally weak relationships across drivers. Incoming longwave radiation (LW_IN) was negatively associated with ET and T, highlighting its influence on surface energy balance and water flux partitioning.

Discussion
This study demonstrates that carbon and water fluxes in the thorn scrub ecosystem of Bosque Fray Jorge are strongly regulated by water availability and the occurrence of precipitation pulses associated with interannual climate variability. Across three hydrological years, rainfall pulses consistently triggered rapid increases in ecosystem respiration (RECO), followed by increases in gross primary productivity (GPP), resulting in more negative net ecosystem exchange (NEE), together with intensified transpiration. This pulse–response behavior is characteristic of arid and semi-arid ecosystems, where biological activity is concentrated in short temporal windows following episodic water inputs (Noy-Meir, 1973; Huxman et al., 2004).
The rainiest years, characterized by high daily sums of photosynthetically active radiation (PAR), showed elevated annual carbon uptake and robust ecosystem productivity, outperforming many Mediterranean and semi-arid shrubland systems despite receiving substantially lower annual precipitation. Annual NEE values in Fray Jorge (−187 to −305 g C m⁻² yr⁻¹; equivalent to −6.8 to −11.2 Mg CO₂ ha⁻¹ yr⁻¹) fall within the upper range reported for arid and semi-arid shrublands globally, reinforcing the idea that ecosystem productivity in water-limited environments depends less on total annual precipitation than on the magnitude, intensity, and timing of rainfall pulses and their synchronization with favorable energy conditions (Bai et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2020).
Functional coupling between vegetation and the environment was particularly evident during pulse-driven wet periods. During these intervals, plants maximized carbon assimilation while limiting non-productive evaporation, with transpiration contributing more than 60% of total evapotranspiration (T/ET > 60%). These responses occurred under relatively low atmospheric evaporative demand, with mean daily vapor pressure deficit around 0.51 +- 0.22 kPa for days when transpiration contributed more than 60% of total evapotranspiration and NEE remained negative. Low VPD conditions are known to reduce atmospheric water demand and maintain favorable leaf-to-air water potential gradients, allowing sustained stomatal opening and enhancing carbon assimilation efficiency while minimizing hydraulic stress (Novick et al., 2016; Grossiord et al., 2020). In coastal drylands such as Fray Jorge, the frequent influence of marine air masses and fog further contributes to maintaining low VPD conditions, effectively buffering atmospheric drought and promoting higher ecohydrological efficiency.
This high efficiency is further supported by plant functional strategies characteristic of the system. The shrubland includes species with contrasting phenological behaviors, including evergreen, drought-deciduous, and semi-deciduous forms, which allow rapid activation following rainfall pulses and flexible responses to water availability. In addition, the presence of species with dimorphic root systems enables access to both shallow soil moisture and deeper water sources. In this context, hydraulic lift has been documented in shrub species from north-central Chile, where water is passively redistributed from deeper moist layers to drier upper horizons, enhancing soil moisture availability and sustaining plant activity during dry periods (Muñoz et al., 2008; Squeo et al., 1999). This process may extend the duration of physiological activity following rainfall events and facilitate continued transpiration and carbon uptake beyond the immediate pulse period.
Furthermore, hydrogeological studies in the region indicate that deep-rooted vegetation may access groundwater or deeper soil moisture reserves, particularly during prolonged dry periods, reducing dependence on surface precipitation (Squeo et al., 2006). In our study, annual evapotranspiration exceeded annual precipitation in all three years, a pattern consistent with possible access to deeper water sources, including deep soil moisture and potential groundwater influence. However, because groundwater uptake was not measured directly, we interpret this mechanism as a plausible ecohydrological explanation rather than direct evidence of groundwater use. This potential deep-water buffering may have contributed to the observed persistence of carbon uptake and the relatively low interannual variability of NEE, even under low precipitation conditions (Squeo & Méndez, 2019).
At the same time, low respiration rates may also play a key role in maintaining negative NEE values. In water-limited ecosystems, soil respiration is strongly constrained by soil moisture, shifting from temperature to moisture limitation during dry periods. Evidence from Mediterranean drylands in central Chile shows that soil respiration declines sharply under low soil moisture due to reduced microbial and root activity (Almagro et al., 2025; Rey et al., 2011). Given the similar climatic conditions to Bosque Fray Jorge, this mechanism likely operates at our site, where reduced Reco during extended dry periods may limit carbon losses. Notably, soil respiration rates reported for dryland shrublands in central Chile are among the lowest observed across sites (Almagro et al., 2025), supporting the idea that constrained respiratory fluxes contribute to the maintenance of negative NEE, while pulse-driven increases in GPP enhance carbon uptake.
In contrast, drier years, or periods associated with La Niña conditions, exhibited reduced GPP and a lower relative contribution of transpiration to evapotranspiration, suggesting partial ecohydrological decoupling. However, absolute magnitudes of transpiration and evaporation did not differ consistently among years, indicating a nuanced response shaped by pulse intensity, surface soil moisture availability, and vegetation phenology (Bonfils et al., 2017; Reich et al., 2024; Carneiro et al., 2025). Overall, these patterns indicate that the Fray Jorge shrubland functioned as a persistent carbon sink during the study period, likely supported by a combination of pulse-driven dynamics, low atmospheric water demand, possible access to deeper water sources, and plant functional adaptations that enhance ecohydrological efficiency under extreme aridity.

The spatial modeling approach complements the eddy covariance observations by extending flux estimates from the tower footprint to the surrounding landscape. While EC measurements capture high-frequency temporal dynamics at a local scale, the remote sensing–based models provide spatially explicit estimates of NEE across the entire shrubland. The consistency in sink magnitude between modeled and tower-derived NEE supports the usefulness of the modeling framework for within-class spatial extrapolation, but the mismatch in interannual ranking indicates limited temporal transferability and should be interpreted cautiously. However, as the spatial modeling is dependent on a single tower, the estimates are likely subject to extrapolation biases, particularly regarding soil processes and plant access to deeper soil moisture. Subsurface lateral movement of water is likely to be highly spatially variable and difficult to capture with meteorological and remote sensing variables.
Despite interannual differences in precipitation, spatial patterns of NEE remained relatively coherent, indicating that structural controls such as vegetation distribution and terrain-adjusted radiation exert a dominant influence on the spatial organization of carbon fluxes. However, interannual variability in flux magnitude was evident, reflecting changes in water availability and pulse dynamics.
The comparison between model configurations further highlights the role of water availability. The statistically optimal model (NDVI + RAD_topo) captured sharper spatial gradients and higher variability, reflecting strong control by vegetation structure and energy inputs. In contrast, the ecohydrological model, which incorporated a soil moisture proxy (Pol_moisture), produced smoother spatial patterns and reduced variability, indicating that moisture availability dampens spatial contrasts in carbon fluxes. This suggests a hierarchical control in which radiation and vegetation define the spatial template of NEE, while soil moisture regulates its temporal variability.
Overall, these findings demonstrate that the Fray Jorge shrubland functions as a persistent and resilient carbon sink, sustained by the interaction of pulse-driven ecohydrological dynamics, low atmospheric water demand, deep water access, and plant functional adaptations. The integration of eddy covariance measurements with remote sensing and machine learning provides a robust framework to disentangle the spatial and temporal controls of carbon fluxes in water-limited ecosystems under increasing climatic variability.
Conclusions
This study demonstrates that the coastal arid thorn scrub of Bosque Fray Jorge functions as a persistent and efficient carbon sink, despite extreme water limitation. Using three years of continuous eddy covariance measurements, we quantified annual carbon and water fluxes and showed that interannual variability in precipitation—expressed as discrete, episodic rainfall pulses—is the dominant driver of ecosystem carbon exchange. Years characterized by effective precipitation pulses supported higher gross primary productivity and more negative net ecosystem exchange, whereas drier years exhibited reduced carbon uptake, although assimilation remained comparatively high for an arid ecosystem.
Beyond interannual patterns, our analyses reveal that ecosystem functioning is governed by well-defined climatic and ecohydrological thresholds. Carbon uptake was activated only when water availability, radiation, and thermal conditions were simultaneously favorable. CART regression identified sharp thresholds in photosynthetically active radiation and soil moisture that regulated daily transitions between carbon sink and near-neutral exchange, as well as thresholds controlling the partitioning of evapotranspiration between transpiration and evaporation. These results highlight that productivity in this arid shrubland is not a gradual response to environmental forcing, but rather emerges within narrow functional windows, underscoring the ecosystem’s sensitivity to hydroclimatic variability and its dependence on the timing and magnitude of rainfall pulses.
By integrating tower-based flux measurements with satellite-derived vegetation indices, terrain-adjusted radiation, and surface moisture proxies, we developed and validated remote sensing–based statistical models capable of upscaling net ecosystem exchange to the landscape scale. The Support Vector Regression (SVR) models provided robust predictions of spatial NEE variability (R² ≈ 0.55), confirming that tower observations are broadly representative of the surrounding shrubland. The comparison between model configurations further revealed that radiation and vegetation structure primarily define the spatial template of carbon fluxes, while soil moisture exerts a secondary but stabilizing control on spatial variability.
Spatial predictions revealed a coherent and persistent carbon sink across the landscape, with most areas exhibiting negative NEE values and only limited zones acting as net sources. Interannual variability was evident but spatially structured, with environmentally sensitive areas showing higher variability across years. The ecohydrological model produced smoother spatial patterns, highlighting the role of water availability in dampening spatial contrasts in carbon fluxes.
Overall, these results demonstrate that arid shrublands can sustain substantial carbon sequestration when episodic water inputs align with favorable radiation conditions and low atmospheric water demand, and when vegetation and soil constraints limit respiratory losses. The combination of pulse-driven productivity, reduced respiration during extended dry periods, and efficient water use allows Bosque Fray Jorge to maintain a persistent carbon sink comparable to or exceeding that of less arid Mediterranean systems.
These findings emphasize the need to explicitly represent arid and hyper-arid shrublands in regional and global carbon budgets and highlight the value of combining eddy covariance observations with remote sensing and machine learning to capture both temporal dynamics and spatial heterogeneity. As climate variability intensifies, identifying ecohydrological thresholds and understanding their role in regulating carbon and water fluxes will be critical for anticipating functional shifts and informing conservation and management strategies in dryland ecosystems.
